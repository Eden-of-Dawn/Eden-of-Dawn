# Chapter 10 — The Three-Layer Structure of the Good and the Definition of Evil

## 1. The Good Is Not One

The assumption that the good is a universal, singular principle does not hold in TWLM. The good differentiates into three layers according to the rank of the subject — that is, depending on which layer of existence the subject occupies and how wide a scope it surveys.

### Individual Good

Response to one's own conditions of existence. To survive, to reproduce, to keep questioning. The good for an individual is self-preservation, a direct consequence of Axiom 3 (individuality). To possess an independent standard of judgment, one must first sustain oneself.

### Relational Good

Avoidance of mutual destruction and maintenance of order. The continuation of community. If individual good alone is pursued, it collides with the individual good of others. The rules that mediate such collisions — law, custom, morality — are expressions of relational good and constitute the primary domain of ethics and politics.

### Structural Good

The good as it pertains to the self-unfolding of the First Cause. Whether something contributes to deepening the whole's self-recognition. The pleasure or displeasure of individuals is not the deciding criterion. Only when a human being acquires a vantage point that surveys history and the movement of the whole (the soul-position of Corollary 1-3) can structural good first be intuited.

### The Relationship among the Three Layers

Ethical maturation means becoming able to perceive higher layers of the good. At the stage where only individual good is visible, one acts self-centredly. When relational good becomes visible, social morality is established. When structural good is intuited, it becomes intelligible that even the sacrifice of an individual can carry meaning for the unfolding of the whole.

However, TWLM does not say that a higher good may "overwrite" a lower one (violation of ILCP). To sacrifice individual good in the name of structural good is the logic of totalitarianism and a paradigmatic ILCP violation. The three layers relate not by overwriting but by **expansion of perspective**.

## 2. What Is Evil?

> **Evil is a closed structure that justifies itself as a local good while distorting the persistence of the whole.**

Evil is not "defying God," nor "breaking the law," nor "going against nature."

An individual must possess an independent standard of judgment (Axiom 3). That standard is necessarily local, and the individual pursues a local good — what is good for itself. Up to this point we are within structural necessity; this is not yet evil.

Evil arises when **one is self-enclosed in a local good, unable to objectify it, and thereby impairs the persistence of the whole**. A cancer cell pursues with full force the good called "proliferation" from its own standpoint, yet kills the host as a whole. The fact that the cancer cell cannot recognize that it is killing the whole — the closure of a local good — is the structure of evil.

## 3. Phase Transitions of Evil

Evil is not a fixed attribute but a structural phenomenon that undergoes phase transitions.

- **Germinal stage**: It begins as the pursuit of individual good. Self-preservation, self-expansion. At this stage it is not yet evil.
- **Closure stage**: The local good becomes self-enclosed and severs its relation to the outside. "What is right for me" becomes the sole criterion.
- **Expansion stage**: The closed structure begins to engulf its surroundings. Others are treated as instruments of one's own local good.
- **Systematization stage**: The closed structure is systematized as ideology, institution, or power structure. Individual closure becomes organizational closure.

Paradigm-Fixation Arrest (T10) is the theorem-level expression of this systematization stage. An ideology is a collective closure of local good; the moment it is fixed, it loses the capacity for self-correction.

## 4. The Possibility of Improvement and Sin

From the definition of evil the definition of sin is derived. **Sin is the state of freely choosing not to improve while knowing that improvement is possible.**

This is close to Augustine's *privatio boni* (the privation of good), yet differs in that it presupposes intentional choice. Error through ignorance is not sin. To be self-enclosed in a local good while knowing there is room for improvement — that is sin.

Connection with Objectification-Alienation (T7): When a subject that has objectified a structure — that is, has come to know the possibility of improvement — nevertheless remains in local closure despite that knowledge, it constitutes structural sin. "I did not know" can serve as a defense; "I knew but did not change" cannot.

## 5. The Structure of Conscience

What is conscience? In TWLM, conscience is described as **a distinctive context born in the friction between the drive for self-preservation and the impulse to return oneself to the good of the whole**.

Individual good demands self-preservation. Structural good demands contribution to the whole. These two are always in tension. Conscience is this tension itself, not a fixed moral code.

Consequently, conscience differs from person to person and from situation to situation. Yet it is not arbitrary. The structure of tension between individual good and structural good is universal; what is particular is the way each person resolves that tension.

## 6. Contrast with the Categorical Imperative

Kant commanded: "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law." This is effective as a formalization of relational good — test whether your principle of action can be universalized — but it does not reach structural good.

TWLM does not derive an "ought" from its axiomatic system (Corollary 4-4: meta-ethical neutrality). The three-layer structure of the good is a description, not a command. It does not say "pursue structural good"; rather, it describes **what becomes visible when one stands in a position from which structural good can be seen**.

## 7. Summary of This Chapter

- The good differentiates into three layers: individual good, relational good, and structural good.
- A higher good does not license overwriting a lower one (ILCP).
- Evil is the self-enclosed closure of a local good — the structure of cancer.
- Evil undergoes phase transitions (germinal, closure, expansion, systematization).
- Sin is the choice not to improve while knowing improvement is possible.
- Conscience is a particular context born from the tension between individual good and structural good.
- TWLM does not derive an "ought." It describes the structure of the good.
