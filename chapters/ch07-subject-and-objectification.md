# Chapter 7 — Subject and Objectification: Those Who Know Cannot Return

## 1. What the Subject Can See

> **T4 (Subject Objectification Range): The subject can objectify only the structural layers one level above and one level below the layer on which it is constituted.**

The human subject can objectify the constraints that constitute it (the lower layer: body, matter) and the ideas it references (the upper layer: meaning, culture). But layers beyond these — for instance, physical laws at the subatomic-particle level or structural good on a cosmic scale — cannot be objectified directly.

The world visible from the subject is therefore necessarily three-layered (lower, self, upper). Adding the soul (Corollary 1-3) as the position that surveys from outside the axiomatic system, the structure of the human agent comprises four layers.

## 2. The Subject Cannot Fully Objectify Itself

> **T6 (Subject's Self-Objectification Impossibility): The subject cannot fully objectify itself. The moment it does, what results is not the subject but meaning (a description of the objectified structure).**

This theorem is intuitively easy to grasp. You cannot see your own eyes directly with your own eyes. A mirror shows you an image, but the image is not the eyes themselves. Likewise, when the subject "objectifies" itself, the result is a model of the subject (a description one level down), not the subject itself.

The consequences are significant:

- If spirit can be objectified, then the subject that objectifies it is not spirit.
- If the soul can be conceptually objectified, then it is at least not the soul as substance.
- When you speak about "yourself," the "yourself" being spoken of is not the "yourself" doing the speaking.

This is both the theorem-level expression of Kant's critique of the paralogisms — the error of mistaking the unity of subjective consciousness for a substance called "soul" — and the ground on which TWLM defines the soul **not as substance but as position**.

## 3. One Cannot Return to an Objectified Structure

> **T7 (Objectification-Alienation): A subject that has objectified a meaning-structure cannot re-immerse itself at the same layer within that structure. If it does immerse, the act becomes performance and ceases to be its own meaning.**

This is one of the most practically consequential theorems in this book.

Suppose a person who believed in a religion has objectified that religion's structure. They have come to understand under what historical conditions its doctrines were formed, what psychological functions they served, and what social structures they sustained. Can that person "return" to the original faith?

Formally, it is possible. They can attend church, recite prayers, participate in rituals. But this is **performance**. It is no longer possible to be unconsciously immersed within an objectified structure. Behind the self that "believes," there always stands the self who is performing belief.

The same thing happens with political convictions, a sense of belonging to a nation, ethnic identity, professional self-image — every meaning-structure whatsoever.

**Those who have come to know cannot return to the state before knowing.**

This is a loss, but structurally it is also a liberation. One moves from functioning unconsciously within a meaning-structure to a position from which that structure can be treated as raw material. In Heidegger's terminology, this corresponds to the transition from "falling" to "authenticity," but TWLM makes explicit that this transition is irreversible.

## 4. Objectification of Context Is the Condition of Escape

> **T8 (Context-Objectification Escape): A subject that has objectified its context becomes capable of escaping unconscious domination by that context. However, the objectifying subject is itself always already inside some other context.**

The condition for escaping ideological domination is to objectify that ideology. The condition for escaping the contextual domination of media is to recognise that media constitute one's context in the first place. The condition for escaping the constraints of language is to be able to treat word-meanings as objects of manipulation (discussed in detail in Chapter 9).

T8, however, carries an important caveat. **The objectifying subject is itself always already inside some other context.** No fully meta-position exists. A subject that has objectified context A is inside context B. If it objectifies context B, it is inside context C.

This is not an infinite regress but a **recursive ascent**. At each stage the scope of what can be objectified widens, yet one never arrives at "an outside from which everything has been objectified." As Corollary 0-3 states, it is acknowledged that an unexplained remainder exists outside the axiomatic system.

## 5. Three Stages of Epistemic Development

Integrating T6, T7, and T8, the epistemic development of the subject can be organised into three stages.

| Stage | State | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Immersion | Unconsciously existing inside a meaning-structure | Context = self. One cannot distinguish whether a question is spontaneous or implanted |
| 2. Objectification | Capable of observing the meaning-structure from outside | Context ≠ self. Those who know cannot return (T7). The meaning-structure becomes raw material |
| 3. Manipulation | Treating meaning-structures as designed artefacts | Word-meanings are used as operators. Contexts can be recombined. Yet no complete outside exists |

Stage 1 is the everyday condition of most human beings and corresponds to Heidegger's das Man (the "They"). The transition to Stage 2 is the central objective of this book, and Stage 3 is the precondition for Chapter 15 ("Do What Thou Wilt").

## 6. Ethical Consequences of Objectification

T7 (Objectification-Alienation) carries ethical consequences.

To re-immerse oneself in an objectified meaning-structure and pretend "not to have known" is structurally **performance**, and more strongly put, a **lie**. Following an ideology after having learned the axiomatic system is qualitatively different from following it in ignorance.

From this follows the "revocation of immunity" discussed in Chapter 15. "I did not know" can serve as grounds for exculpation, but "I knew and yet followed" cannot. This is TWLM's re-grounding of the Nuremberg Principle — that superior orders do not constitute grounds for exculpation.

## 7. Summary of This Chapter

- The subject can objectify only the layers one above and one below (T4).
- The subject cannot fully objectify itself. The objectified self is always a model one level down (T6).
- One cannot unconsciously return to an objectified structure. Those who know cannot return to the state before knowing (T7).
- Objectification of context is the condition of escape from domination. Yet no complete outside exists (T8).
- Epistemic development proceeds through three stages: immersion, objectification, manipulation.
- Pretending "not to have known" after objectification is structurally a lie, and forms the foundation for the revocation of immunity.
